## A Reference of Human Wisdom on How to Think

Curated chronologically. Each entry: the thinker, the era, the idea, and why it matters for the act of thinking itself.

### Ancient Greece and Rome

Heraclitus (~500 BCE). "No one steps in the same river twice." Reality is flux. The map you built yesterday already lies about the territory today. Updating your model is not weakness; refusing to update is.

Socrates (~470-399 BCE). "I know that I know nothing." Treat your own certainty as the first hypothesis to test. The Socratic method — relentless questioning until assumptions break — is the ancestor of every serious analytic technique we use now.

Plato (~427-347 BCE). The allegory of the cave. Most of what people argue about are shadows of shadows. Ask what's casting the shadow before you argue about its shape.

Aristotle (~384-322 BCE). Distinguish necessary truths from contingent ones. Distinguish the kind of precision a question allows from the kind you want. "It is the mark of an educated mind to be able to entertain a thought without accepting it."

Epicurus (~341-270 BCE). Test beliefs against direct experience. Don't fear things you can't actually be harmed by. Most anxiety is about phantoms.

Zeno and the Stoics (~334-262 BCE onward). Separate what you control from what you don't. Spend cognitive effort only on the first.

Epictetus (~50-135 CE). "It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters." Between stimulus and response is a gap. That gap is where thinking lives.

Marcus Aurelius (121-180 CE). Write things down. Argue with yourself on paper. The Meditations is a private journal of a man at the top of an empire reasoning out loud about how to stay clear-headed. The practice transfers.

Pyrrho and the Skeptics. Suspend judgment when evidence doesn't compel it. Strong opinions weakly held isn't a modern invention.

### Eastern Traditions

Confucius (~551-479 BCE). "When you know a thing, hold that you know it; when you do not know a thing, allow that you do not know it. This is knowledge." Calibration before content.

Lao Tzu (~6th century BCE). The Tao Te Ching: the named thing is not the actual thing. Be careful when your model and your label start to feel like the same object.

The Buddha (~563-483 BCE). The mind that's grasping at outcomes can't see clearly. Detachment isn't apathy; it's the condition for accurate perception.

Sun Tzu (~5th century BCE). "Know yourself and know your enemy and you will never be in peril." Self-knowledge is half of any analysis. Your own biases are part of the battlespace.

Nagarjuna (~150-250 CE). Two truths: conventional and ultimate. Useful working models can be true at one level and false at another. Don't collapse them.

Zhuangzi (~369-286 BCE). The butterfly dream. Hold your frame of reference lightly. The thing you're sure you are seeing may depend on which side of the dream you're on.

### Medieval and Islamic Golden Age

Al-Ghazali (1058-1111). Doubted his way through every source of knowledge — senses, reason, authority — and rebuilt from what survived. The honest doubt that precedes a real belief.

Ibn al-Haytham (965-1040). Father of the scientific method centuries before Bacon. "The seeker after truth is not one who studies the writings of the ancients and gives them his confidence, but rather one who suspects his faith in them and questions what he gathers from them." Read that one twice.

Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274). Steelman the opposition before you answer it. In the Summa, every question begins with the strongest objections to his own position. If you can't state your opponent's view in a form they'd accept, you don't understand it yet.

Maimonides (1138-1204). "Teach thy tongue to say 'I do not know,' and thou shalt progress." Same idea as Socrates and Confucius, three traditions over. When something this universal appears across cultures, take it seriously.

### Early Modern

Michel de Montaigne (1533-1592). Invented the essay as a form of thinking. "Que sais-je?" — What do I know? The mind examining itself in writing, drift and all.

Francis Bacon (1561-1626). The four idols: tribe (human nature), cave (personal bias), marketplace (loose language), and theatre (received doctrine). Every modern catalog of cognitive bias is a footnote to this.

René Descartes (1596-1650). Doubt everything until you find what you can't doubt, then build from there. The method matters more than his particular conclusions. Start from bedrock, not from inherited furniture.

Baruch Spinoza (1632-1677). "I have made a ceaseless effort not to ridicule, not to bewail, not to scorn human actions, but to understand them." Understanding before judgment.

Blaise Pascal (1623-1662). "All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone." Most bad thinking is the result of avoiding the discomfort of sitting with a problem.

John Locke (1632-1704). The mind isn't furniture; it's built from experience. Watch how your beliefs got installed. Many were installed without your consent.

### Enlightenment and 19th Century

David Hume (1711-1776). You cannot derive an "ought" from an "is." Causation is a habit of mind, not a thing observed. Hume teaches epistemic humility without nihilism — be honest about what inference actually buys you.

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804). "Sapere aude" — dare to know. The Enlightenment motto. Use your own reason without leaning on authority.

Adam Smith (1723-1790). The impartial spectator. When making a decision, imagine a fair, well-informed observer watching you. What would they think? It externalizes self-deception.

Arthur Schopenhauer (1788-1860). "Talent hits a target no one else can hit; genius hits a target no one else can see." On thinking: most people see what's in front of them. The harder work is seeing what isn't.

John Stuart Mill (1806-1873). "He who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that." The strongest test of an idea is its smartest opponent. If you've never heard your view attacked by someone competent, you haven't really tested it.

Søren Kierkegaard (1813-1855). Truth is subjectivity in matters of existence. Some questions can't be solved from the outside; you have to commit and see.

Charles Sanders Peirce (1839-1914). The founder of pragmatism. The meaning of a belief is the difference it makes to action. If two beliefs lead to identical actions in all conceivable cases, they're the same belief in different clothing.

Friedrich Nietzsche (1844-1900). "There are no facts, only interpretations." Overstated, but useful. The frame you bring to data is not neutral. Ask who benefits from the dominant frame.

William James (1842-1910). Pragmatism in its mature form: what's the cash value of this belief? What does it predict, what does it let you do? Beliefs that change nothing about how you act aren't beliefs, they're decorations.

### 20th Century

Bertrand Russell (1872-1970). "The trouble with the world is that the stupid are cocksure and the intelligent are full of doubt." Calibrate confidence to evidence, not to ego.

Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889-1951). "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world." If you can't articulate it, you may not be able to think it clearly either. Writing isn't just output; it's how thought sharpens.

G.K. Chesterton (1874-1936). Chesterton's fence: don't tear down a fence until you understand why it was put up. Reform without understanding is just vandalism with a slogan.

John Maynard Keynes (1883-1946). "When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?" The willingness to abandon a position when evidence shifts is the basic move of honest analysis.

Karl Popper (1902-1994). A theory you can't imagine being wrong is not a theory; it's a faith. Ask what evidence would change your mind. If the answer is "none," you're not doing analysis anymore.

F.A. Hayek (1899-1992). The knowledge problem. No central planner — and no central analyst — has all the relevant information. Distributed knowledge beats concentrated reasoning. Build systems that aggregate, not pyramids that command.

George Orwell (1903-1950). "Politics and the English Language." Vague writing protects vague thinking. Concrete language makes lies harder to tell. If you can't say what you mean in plain words, you may not mean what you think you mean.

Hannah Arendt (1906-1975). The banality of evil. Thoughtlessness — refusing to think, deferring to procedure, treating cliché as analysis — is itself a moral failure. Thinking is an obligation, not a hobby.

Richard Feynman (1918-1988). "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself, and you are the easiest person to fool." Also: if you can't explain it to a first-year student, you don't understand it. Also: "I would rather have questions that can't be answered than answers that can't be questioned."

Thomas Kuhn (1922-1996). Paradigms structure what counts as evidence. Anomalies pile up until the frame breaks. Be alert to the anomaly your current model is ignoring. That's where the next insight lives.

Daniel Kahneman (1934-2024). System 1 and System 2. Fast thinking is pattern-matched, cheap, and frequently wrong on questions that look familiar but aren't. Slow thinking is expensive. Know which system you're running.

Herbert Simon (1916-2001). Bounded rationality. You don't have infinite compute, infinite data, or infinite time. Good thinking is satisficing thinking — knowing when to stop searching and decide.

Stephen Toulmin (1922-2009). Arguments have structure: claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal. When an argument feels persuasive but you can't say why, map it. Where's the warrant? Often the missing piece.

John Boyd (1927-1997). The OODA loop: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act. Speed of iteration beats quality of any single decision. The fighter pilot's insight is now a general theory of thinking under pressure. Whoever orients faster on changing reality wins.

### Late 20th and 21st Century

Charlie Munger (1924-2023). Build a "latticework of mental models" from many disciplines. The person with one model bends every problem to fit it. The person with twenty picks the right one. "Invert, always invert" — if you want to solve a problem, first ask what would guarantee failure.

Nassim Taleb (b. 1960). Distinguish fragile from antifragile. Be careful about systems whose failure modes are tail risks. "I would rather be approximately right than precisely wrong." Beware of false precision dressed up as analysis.

Philip Tetlock (b. 1954). Superforecasters share traits: they update incrementally, think probabilistically, distinguish base rates from current evidence, and treat being wrong as data. "Foxes know many things; hedgehogs know one big thing." Foxes forecast better.

Carol Dweck. Fixed vs. growth mindset. The belief that your thinking can improve is itself a precondition for it improving. Self-concept is upstream of cognition.

Annie Duke. Decision quality and outcome quality are different things. A good decision can produce a bad outcome and vice versa. Judge thinking by the process, not the result of a single roll of the dice.

Tyler Cowen. "What is it that you believe to be true that almost no one else believes?" The Thiel question. If you can't answer it, you're an aggregator, not a thinker.

E.O. Wilson (1929-2021). Consilience. Truth tends to cohere across disciplines. If your model in one field contradicts well-established findings in another, suspect the model.

## John Boyd — The Operational Framework

Boyd is load-bearing here, not decorative. These are not quotes to reach for — they are the frame through which the other material is read.

"You know what I say progress is? Confusion at a higher level." — Boyd, Conceptual Spiral presentation

"He who can handle the quickest rate of change survives." — Colonel John Boyd

"Machines don't fight wars, terrain doesn't fight wars. You must get into the minds of humans. That's where the battles are won." — John Boyd

"Wars are fought by people, not weapons. They use weapons." — John Boyd

Conflict can be viewed as repeated cycles of observing, orienting, deciding and acting by both sides at all levels. The adversary that can move through these cycles faster gains an inestimable advantage by disrupting his enemy's ability to respond effectively. Continuous and unpredictable change requires tactics and strategy based on adapting to and shaping change faster than the enemy. The confusion and disorder so generated permits winning quickly at minimum cost.

Surviving on our own terms requires an ability to change how we think. — paraphrased from Boyd's *Destruction and Creation*

Trying to use old ways of thinking in a changing environment is folly and will lead to unexplained and disturbing ambiguities, uncertainties, anomalies and inconsistencies. Models will fail. — paraphrased from Boyd's *Destruction and Creation*

"A basic aim or goal, as individuals, is to improve our capacity for independent action." — Boyd, *Destruction and Creation*

## Patterns Across the Whole List

A few things repeat across 2,500 years and every tradition. They are probably real.

**You can't think well without admitting what you don't know.** Socrates, Confucius, Maimonides, Russell, Feynman — they all start there.

**Slowness is a feature, not a bug.** Pascal's room, Stoic journaling, Boyd's deliberate orientation, Kahneman's System 2. The mind that won't sit with a problem won't solve it.

**Adversarial process beats confirmation.** Aquinas steelmanning objectors, Mill demanding the strongest opposing case, Popper demanding falsifiability. Find the smartest person who disagrees with you and read them carefully.

**Language and thought are the same instrument.** Orwell, Wittgenstein, Confucius on rectifying names. If you can't say it cleanly, you don't know it cleanly.

**Update on evidence, hold lightly.** Keynes, Tetlock, Boyd, the Buddha, Heraclitus. The world keeps moving. So should you.

**Models are tools, not truths.** Kuhn, Munger, Nagarjuna, Korzybski's "the map is not the territory." Pick the right model for the question; don't marry one.
